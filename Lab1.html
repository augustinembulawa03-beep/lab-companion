Overview

In Lab 1 you will learn how to measure electrical behaviour using three key instruments: the KEITHLEY 2110 DMM, the Tektronix TBS 2000B oscilloscope, and the Tektronix AFG1022 function generator.

This matters because every later lab depends on accurate measurement setup (correct ports, correct probe attenuation, and correct series/parallel connections) and on interpreting waveforms using Vpp, T, and frequency.

Safety & Common Mistakes

  • ⚠ Use the correct DMM socket and lead set before measuring (voltage vs current ports differ).
  • ⚠ When measuring current, connect the DMM in series (never in parallel like a voltmeter).
  • ⚠ Never measure resistance on a powered circuit; disconnect power first.
  • ⚠ Match the scope channel/probe attenuation setting (1X vs 10X) before trusting Vpp.

Theory Mini-Lesson

2–4 minute read – key ideas only

1) KEITHLEY 2110 DMM: current vs voltage

The DMM measures differently depending on what you connect it to. For current measurements, the meter must be placed in series so the current flows through the meter. For voltage measurements, the meter is effectively a high-impedance device and is used in parallel across two nodes.

2) Tektronix TBS 2000B: reading Vpp and period

On the oscilloscope you will measure peak-to-peak voltage (Vpp) and the period (T). The frequency comes directly from the period: f = 1/T.

You also need to pay attention to probe attenuation. If the probe is set to 10X but the channel is left at 1X (or vice versa), the scope will display the wrong amplitude.

3) AFG1022 function generator: verify the signal on CH2

The function generator provides a reference waveform so you can compare the configured frequency with what you measure on the oscilloscope. You will set a 1 kHz, 2.5 V sine wave and confirm it using CH2 readings.

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Task 1 (DMM): Identify components using lead-swap behaviour
    Use the KEITHLEY 2110 to measure the resistance of the supplied components. For each component, record the reading with the DMM leads connected both ways (swap leads).
    Hint: if the resistance is very different depending on lead direction, the component is likely diode-like.

    Optional check: if you have an LED available, use the DMM diode-test function to measure its forward voltage (place probes across the LED and record the forward-bias reading).

    Common mistake: Students often forget to connect the power supply ground to the breadboard ground rail. Without a shared reference the LED will not light even if everything else is correct.
    Expected Result: Forward voltage across LED should typically be between 1.8 V and 2.2 V depending on LED colour.
    If voltage is close to 0 V, check polarity or wiring.
    If voltage equals supply voltage, LED may be disconnected or open circuit.
  2. Task 2 (Oscilloscope): Measure Vpp and period from a 5 V square wave
    Using the Tektronix TBS 2000B, view the supplied 5 V square wave and measure: Vpp and period (T). Calculate frequency using f = 1/T.
    Hint: set the scope/probe attenuation correctly (1X vs 10X). Then intentionally mismatch it and observe how the measured Vpp becomes wrong.
  3. Task 3 (Function generator): Set 1 kHz, 2.5 V sine wave and verify on CH2
    Configure the AFG1022 to output a 1 kHz, 2.5 V sine wave. Connect/observe it on scope CH2, then compare the observed frequency with your calculation from the oscilloscope.
    Hint: if CH2 looks offset or the amplitude seems off, re-check your probe attenuation and coupling settings.

Data Capture & Calculations

Record the measurements you will need for later labs:

  • Component identification data: R with DMM leads in direction A to B and B to A.
  • Square-wave scope data: Vpp and period T, then f = 1/T.
  • Sine-wave verification: compare the AFG1022 frequency setting with your scope-derived frequency.
Lab tip: If the LED does not turn on but wiring looks correct, measure voltage across the resistor first. This quickly confirms whether current is flowing in the circuit.
